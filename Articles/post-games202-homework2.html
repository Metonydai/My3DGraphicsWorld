WebGL Precomputed Radiance Transfer (PRT)

Introduction

This project implements a real-time Precomputed Radiance Transfer (PRT) rendering pipeline. Leveraging Nori to precompute environment cubemap lighting and mesh transport data into Spherical Harmonics coefficients, it achieves dynamic, high-fidelity real-time global illumination and soft shadowing for complex OBJ models directly within a WebGL frame.

Rendering Equation

$$L_{o}(p, \omega_{o}) = L_{e}(p, \omega_{o}) + \int_{\Omega^{+}}^{}L_{i}(p, \omega_{i})f_{r}(p, \omega_{i}, \omega_{o})\cos\theta_{i}{d\omega_{i}}$$

Where:
  • \(L_{o}(p, \omega_{o})\) : The radiance in the outgoing direction \(\omega_{o}\) for the shading point p
  • \(L_{e}(p, \omega_{o})\) : Emitted radiance term (Self-emission)
  • \({\Omega^{+}}\) : Integration domain, integrating only over the upper hemisphere of the surface
  • \(L_{i}(p,\omega_{i})\) : Lighting, the radiance from a specific incoming direction \(\omega_{i}\)
  • \(f_{r}(p, \omega_{i}, \omega_{o})\) : BRDF, describing the contribution of light from a specific incoming direction to the outgoing direction.
  • \(\cos\theta_{i}\) : The cosine of the angle between the surface normal and a specific incoming direction
The core philosophy relies on solving the classic rendering equation by pre-baking the transport visibility factors of the scene geometry. This breaks down the complex runtime integral into a simple vector dot product.

Spherical Harmonics (SH)

Basis function: A set of mutually independent basis whose projection onto one other is zero.
Given any function \(f(x)\), which can be represented as linear combination of sph basis function. $$ \begin{aligned} f(x) &= \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} \sum_{m=-l}^{l} c_l^m \cdot Y_l^m(\omega_i) \\ &= c_0^0 Y_0^0(\omega_i) + c_{-1}^1 Y_{-1}^1(\omega_i) + c_0^1 Y_0^1(\omega_i) + \dots \end{aligned} $$ For low-frequency functions, approximating with only the first few terms can yield adequate results. If the BRDF is rough, it acts as a low-pass filter.

PRT (Precomputed Radiance Transfer)

Spherical harmonics use a set of basis functions to fit the Lighting term in the rendering equation. If shadowing is required, a visibility term \(V(p, \omega_i)\) needs to be added: $$L_o(p, \omega_o) = \int_{\Omega^{+}} \underbrace{L_i(p, \omega_i)}_{\text{Lighting}} \underbrace{f_r(p, \omega_i, \omega_o)}_{\text{BRDF}} \underbrace{V(p, \omega_i)}_{\text{Visibility}} \max(0, \cos \theta_i) \, d\omega_i$$ In 2002, Sloan proposed PRT (Precomputed Radiance Transfer), which divides the interior of the integral into two parts: Lighting and Light Transport: $$L_o(p, \omega_o) = \int_{\Omega^{+}} \underbrace{L_i(p, \omega_i)}_{\text{Lighting}} \underbrace{f_r(p, \omega_i, \omega_o) V(p, \omega_i) \max(0, \cos \theta_i)}_{\text{Light Transport}} \, d\omega_i$$ Assuming the BRDF term is diffuse, the BRDF term becomes a constant \(\frac{\rho}{\pi}\). By representing Lighting in the form of coefficients and basis functions, and then swapping the order of integration and summation, the interior of the integral can once again be expressed as a projection onto the basis functions: $$ \begin{aligned} L(p, \omega_o) &= \frac{\rho}{\pi} \int_{\Omega^{+}} \sum l_i B_i(\omega_i) V(p, \omega_i) \max(0, \cos \theta_i) \, d\omega_i \\ &\approx \frac{\rho}{\pi} \sum l_i \underbrace{\int_{\Omega^{+}} B_i(\omega_i) V(p, \omega_i) \max(0, \cos \theta_i) \, d\omega_i}_{\text{Precomputed}} \\ &= \frac{\rho}{\pi} \sum l_i T_i \end{aligned} $$ Where \(l_i\) is a set of coefficients from projecting the Lighting term onto the basis functions, and \(T_i\) is the coefficient from projecting the Light Transport term onto the basis functions. This transforms the integral of the product of multiple functions into a simple dot product.However, these precomputations require a few prerequisites:

  • Visibility is fixed: This requires that objects within the scene cannot move.
  • Lighting is "fixed": The components of the lighting cannot change dynamically, though the lighting itself can be rotated (which corresponds to rotating the linear space spanned by the basis functions).
This is highly reasonable, just like how baked lighting information is inherently static.
